How the Model Improves on Halifax HPI

This report shows — in numbers — why a regional house price index is not sufficient for individual loan collateral assessment, how the local factor model corrects for that, and by exactly how much. Based on 914,666 repeat-sale pairs from 2019–2025.

1

The Limitation of Regional HPI Alone

The Halifax index tracks house prices at Government Office Region level — 10 broad regions covering England and Wales. Every property in the South East gets the same index uplift whether it sits in a thriving commuter village or a structurally weak coastal town. Over a five-year horizon, this regional averaging introduces material error at the individual loan level: two properties next door can have very different price trajectories, and the regional index captures neither.

What “close to 50%” means
A regional index is symmetric around the truth — it over-predicts roughly as often as it under-predicts individual sale prices. That is the baseline the local factor model must improve on. A figure near 50% is expected; it is not a failure of the HPI — it is the fundamental limitation of any regional average applied to an individual property.
The haircut consequence
To guarantee an 85% floor, a blanket 10.0% reduction must be applied to every property equally — regardless of postcode, property type, or local market conditions. This is blunt: the same discount applies to a prime London flat and a rural cottage alike.

Dispersion factor
When a sector’s price ratios show high variance, the local factor is less reliable. Dispersion shrinks the prediction back toward the regional HPI when the signal is noisy. Only fires when local factor > 1.0 — it never artificially amplifies downside.
Local factor cap (1.15×)
A data-quality guard: prevents sectors with thin data from generating unreliably high factors. Applies to upside only — not a conservatism mechanism. Downside predictions are uncapped.

The counter-intuitive number

The full model shows 56.5% pre-haircut coverage versus 52.2% for HPI-only. Does that mean HPI is better? No.

Pre-haircut coverage measures how often the model under-predicts. A model predicting £0 has 100% coverage — and would be worthless. The local factors push predictions closer to actual prices (median error shifts from 0.9943 to 0.9834), reducing the built-in downward bias. That is more accuracy, not less.

The right metric

How large a haircut is needed to hit 85% confidence? A more accurate model needs a smaller haircut because its errors are more compact.

Where local factors add the most value
HPI applies the same regional index to every property in a region. Local factors correctly distinguish a structurally declining sector from a resilient one next door. For individual loan assessment — especially near regional boundaries or in areas of structural divergence — this matters far more than the aggregate comparison suggests.
Honest assessment
At the portfolio level, the required aggregate haircut is broadly similar (10.0% vs 8.8%). The model’s advantage is precision at the loan level — avoiding over-discounting good collateral and under-discounting weak collateral.

The 85% Confidence Floor

The confidence haircut is derived empirically: for each property type, find the multiplier h such that P(h × full-model estimate ≤ actual market price) = 85%. Equivalently, h = 1 ÷ (85th percentile of model/actual error ratio). Haircuts are calibrated separately per type because property-level error distributions are materially different between houses and flats.

Why flats need a deeper haircut
Flats (13.9% reduction) require materially more than houses (7.0–7.8%). The flat price distribution is structurally wider, driven by leasehold complexity, service charge variability, and post-Grenfell cladding risk. This is a genuine market feature visible in the repeat-sale data — not a modelling assumption.
Why per-type, not one global haircut
A single global haircut would either under-protect flats (coverage well below 85%) or over-penalise houses (excessive discount on low-risk collateral). Per-type calibration achieves the 85% floor for each type independently, using only what the data provides.

Out-of-Sample Validation (2022–2025)

The model was calibrated on repeat sales up to end-2021 and tested on unseen transactions from 2022 onwards — a period including the post-pandemic price plateau and the 2022–2023 rate-driven correction. This is the most demanding test: local factors must generalise to a market regime not seen during calibration.
