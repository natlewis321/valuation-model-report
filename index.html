Basel 3.1 — SA-CR Collateral Assessment

Prudential Property Valuation Model
Methodology & Performance Report

This report sets out the design of the collateral valuation model, documents the evidence base used to calibrate it, and presents independent validation results. The model is used for two purposes: the five-year collateral backstop under Basel 3.1 SA-CR, and initial desktop valuations for product switch assessments. In both cases the requirement is a prudent, conservatively assessed value — not a market-optimistic estimate.

1

How the Model Works

The model estimates a property's current market value from its original purchase price using four sequential adjustments. Each factor has a specific, empirically justifiable role. The final step applies a per-type confidence haircut calibrated so that the output value is at or below the actual market price in 85% of cases — the prudential floor.

Why Full-Model Coverage Appears Lower Than HPI-Only

A natural question arises from the Section 3 figures: if the full model shows 54.4% pre-haircut coverage versus 57.1% for HPI-only, does that mean the local factors are making the model worse? The answer is no — but it requires understanding what "coverage at the point estimate" actually measures.

The misleading metric

Coverage at 1.0 (no haircut) measures how often the model under-predicts actual prices. A model that always predicted £0 would have 100% coverage at 1.0 — and be entirely useless. High pre-haircut coverage simply means the model has a systematic downward bias, not that it is more accurate.

The local factors are net uplifting across the book — they push predictions closer to actual prices (median error moves from 0.9810 to 0.9883). This reduces coverage at 1.0 because predictions that were safely below actual prices now sit closer to them — but that is a sign of more accurate prediction, not worse.

The right metric: required haircut

The correct question is: given a target confidence level (85%), how large a haircut is needed? A better model needs a smaller haircut because its errors are more compact.

At the aggregate portfolio level, the full model's local factors require a marginally larger haircut than HPI-only (9.5% vs 8.9%). The distribution spread is nearly identical. The local factors shift predictions toward actual prices — more accurate — but also reduce the in-built downward bias of a regional index.
Where local factors add value
The benefit is loan-level differentiation. HPI-only applies the same South East index to every property in the region regardless of postcode. Local factors correctly distinguish a structurally declining sector from a resilient one next door — critical for individual loan assessment even if the aggregate distribution looks similar.

Why Three Model Layers? The Role of the Dispersion Factor

The model has three adjustment mechanisms before the confidence haircut: the postcode factor, the property type factor, and the dispersion factor. A reasonable question is whether these represent three independent layers of conservatism that stack up excessively — and whether the dispersion factor in particular earns its place.

Cap (1.15x)
Data quality guard, not conservatism. Prevents thin-data sectors (few repeat-sale pairs) from generating unreliable high factors. Applies only to upside — downside predictions are not capped.
Dispersion factor
Noise correction, not conservatism. When a sector's pairs show high variance, the average factor is less reliable. Dispersion shrinks the prediction towards HPI when the local signal is noisy. Only fires when the local factor is above 1.0.
Confidence haircut
This is the conservatism. The only mechanism explicitly targeting the 85% confidence floor. Its size depends directly on the quality of the point estimate — better point estimate means smaller haircut needed.

The dispersion factor was stress-tested by computing what the model looks like with and without it across all 955,667 test pairs. The results show it is doing genuine, measurable work — and that removing it would require the haircut to increase to compensate.

The total prudence in the model is the same either way; the dispersion factor simply achieves part of it more precisely. Removing it would make the model slightly blunter and less differentiated, not materially more or less conservative in aggregate.

7

85% Confidence Floor

The confidence haircut is the final step. It is derived empirically: for each property type, we find the value h such that applying it to the full model output gives P(floor value ≤ actual market value) = 85% across the test population. Equivalently, h = 1 ÷ (85th percentile of the model/actual error ratio) for that property type.

Flats require a materially deeper haircut because their price distribution is structurally wider — driven by leasehold complexity, service charge variability, and post-Grenfell cladding risk. This is a genuine market feature, not a modelling artefact; the data reflects it clearly across both HPI sources.
